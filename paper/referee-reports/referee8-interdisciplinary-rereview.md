Recommendation: Major revision

Main remaining concerns

1. The paper is much improved, but the central contribution is still slightly overloaded. The revised manuscript now has a clearer estimand and a better labor-and-tax versus macro-and-trade split, which is a real advance. Still, the paper continues to mix three different stories that do not fully cohere into one broad-audience article: a measurement protocol for prompt-conditioned responses, a descriptive cross-model comparison, and a methodological cautionary result about prompt sensitivity. All three are interesting, but the manuscript still tries to give them near-equal weight. For a broad economics / policy / computational social science audience, I would want a clearer choice of which of these is the headline contribution and which are supporting results. This tension remains visible in the abstract and opening contribution claims in [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L10), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L12), and [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L29).

2. The labor-and-tax versus macro-and-trade split is the right move, but the macro-and-trade side still feels underpowered and less persuasive. The labor-and-tax subpanel is now the substantive core of the paper: it has benchmark comparisons, a policy mapping, and a clear interpretation. The macro-and-trade subpanel, by contrast, has only three quantities, much looser intervals, and no comparable downstream interpretation beyond "ordering changes across domains." That is enough for an interesting descriptive contrast, but not yet enough to carry equal narrative weight. As written, the broad audience may conclude that the paper is really about labor-and-tax elasticities plus a suggestive macro/trade add-on. See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L171), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L182), and [model-overview-macro-trade.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview-macro-trade.md#L1).

3. Prompt sensitivity remains both the most compelling methodological finding and the biggest threat to overinterpretation. The new appendices help a lot: the income sign-fix, Armington clarification, and partial IES clarification all point in the same direction. That strengthens the paper, but it also reinforces that some cross-model disagreement is really object-definition disagreement. The manuscript acknowledges this, but I still think it understates how much this should discipline the headline takeaways. The right broad-audience lesson may be less "models differ systematically in elasticities" and more "measured model differences in economic parameters are highly protocol-sensitive even under a careful design." See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L216), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L224), [income-signfix-delta.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/income-signfix-delta.md#L1), and [armington-clarify-delta.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/armington-clarify-delta.md#L1).

4. The robustness appendix is substantially stronger, but some of it still reads more like internal validation than evidence that will matter to an external reader. The prefix-stability, pooling-robustness, and tool-use checks are sensible and improve confidence in the exercise. However, a broad interdisciplinary reader is unlikely to care much about all of them equally. In particular, the tool-use appendix mostly confirms that the archived arm was barely a tool-using design at all, and some of the technical robustness material risks crowding out the more interpretable substantive results. This is not a problem of doing too much robustness; it is a problem of editorial prioritization. See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L291), [stability-appendix.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/stability-appendix.md#L1), [pooling-robustness-appendix.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/pooling-robustness-appendix.md#L1), and [tool-use-appendix.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/tool-use-appendix.md#L1).

5. The paper still needs one cleaner statement of why a non-specialist should care. The toy top-rate table is a real improvement because it gives the labor-and-tax subpanel a concrete policy translation. But the manuscript still presents this as one result among many rather than as the clearest motivation for the exercise. For a broader audience, the simplest sell is not "here is a protocol for eliciting elasticities from models"; it is "LLMs asked straightforward policy-relevant parameter questions can imply materially different policy conclusions, and those differences are partly domain-specific and partly prompt-sensitive." The paper is now close to that framing, but it should lean into it more aggressively. See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L27), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L200), and [toy-top-rate-labor-tax.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/toy-top-rate-labor-tax.md#L1).

Strengths

1. The paper improved materially relative to the previous round. The revised title, narrower estimand, and explicit framing of the object as prompt-conditioned response distributions are all clear upgrades.

2. The labor-and-tax versus macro-and-trade split is an important improvement and makes the main descriptive claim much more credible.

3. The new robustness appendices substantially strengthen the manuscript. In particular, the stability, pooling, and prompt-clarification checks make this feel like a real measurement paper rather than a one-off prompting exercise.

4. The toy public-finance mapping is exactly the sort of concrete translation the paper needed. It gives the labor-and-tax subpanel a clear policy-facing payoff.

Whether the paper improved relative to the previous round

Yes. The paper is significantly better than in the previous round. It is more disciplined about the estimand, better organized around economically coherent subpanels, and more convincing because the robustness appendix now directly addresses several of the earlier concerns. The paper still needs editorial tightening and a clearer headline contribution, but it is no longer just a promising memo.

Single highest-value next revision

Choose one central takeaway and rewrite the paper around it. My recommendation is to make the labor-and-tax subpanel plus the prompt-sensitivity finding the clear core of the article, and treat the macro-and-trade results as a secondary comparative domain rather than a coequal pillar. That would give the paper a sharper broad-audience identity: LLMs return meaningfully different policy-relevant elasticity distributions, those differences can move simple public-finance conclusions, and some of the disagreement is itself fragile to object-definition clarifications.
