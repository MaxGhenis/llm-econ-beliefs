**Overall Recommendation**

`Reject`

**Major Comments**

1. The paper’s estimand is still not cleanly defined, and the prose slides between a defensible object and a much stronger one. Early on, the paper says it measures “manifested belief” under repeated elicitation [paper.qmd:19](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:19). Later, it speaks as if it has identified model “priors” that would influence policy arguments [paper.qmd:237](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:237), [paper.qmd:241](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:241). Table 3 cuts directly against that stronger interpretation: a small sign clarification moves `grok-4.1-fast` from `+0.09` to `-0.097` on a headline quantity [paper.qmd:225](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:225), [income-signfix-delta.md:14](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/income-signfix-delta.md:14). At present, the paper identifies a prompt-conditioned, sampling-conditioned response distribution, not a stable latent belief object. That is still interesting, but it is a narrower contribution than the paper claims.

2. The headline “responsiveness-heavy” ranking is not economically coherent. Table 1 averages within-quantity ranks across nine very different objects: IES, Armington, capital-labor substitution, labor supply elasticities, ETI, and capital-gains realizations [model-overview.md:3](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview.md:3). The text then interprets this composite as a meaningful model characteristic [paper.qmd:159](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:159) and partially maps it into redistribution claims [paper.qmd:177](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:177). That is not justified. These quantities do not share a common welfare or policy ordering, and the appendix simulation table shows that the rankings are not stable across parameter families anyway [model-overview-simulation.md:3](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview-simulation.md:3). The paper needs to stop treating the average rank as a structurally meaningful summary.

3. The policy relevance is asserted rather than demonstrated. The introduction and interpretation sections repeatedly claim that cross-model parameter differences would map into different redistribution or efficiency conclusions [paper.qmd:27](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:27), [paper.qmd:29](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:29), [paper.qmd:241](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:241). But there is no actual policy calculation. The current evidence is just dispersion in elicited parameter values. For a public-finance journal, that is not enough. The authors need to show at least one explicit mapping from elicited parameters to a policy object: e.g. an implied revenue-maximizing top tax rate, a marginal cost of public funds calculation, or a common microsimulation reform using the PolicyEngine-style elasticities.

4. The evidence for “systematic disagreement” is weaker than the manuscript suggests. Table 2 sorts quantities by spread in pooled centers [quantity-disagreement.md:3](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/quantity-disagreement.md:3), but that is a weak statistic on its own. For Armington, the spread is `1.0` while the mean pooled 90% width is `5.014` [quantity-disagreement.md:6](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/quantity-disagreement.md:6). For ETI, the spread is `0.147` versus a mean width of `1.024` [quantity-disagreement.md:12](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/quantity-disagreement.md:12). More broadly, once one looks beyond point estimates, most canonical quantities exhibit very heavy overlap in model-level intervals; capital gains is the main exception. So the paper is overstating how separated the models are. It needs either stronger separation metrics or a more modest interpretation.

5. Quantity interpretation control remains incomplete, and that undermines the main claims. The paper emphasizes that it fixes the target interpretation in the prompt [paper.qmd:49](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:49), yet the one place where it runs a targeted clarification, the result changes materially [paper.qmd:223](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:223), [income-signfix-delta.md:3](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/income-signfix-delta.md:3). That should make the authors more cautious about the rest of the panel, not less. Table 2 still shows sign-level disagreement on capital gains [quantity-disagreement.md:5](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/quantity-disagreement.md:5), and the largest spreads are precisely on quantities where interpretation is notoriously fragile, such as Armington and IES [quantity-disagreement.md:6](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/quantity-disagreement.md:6), [quantity-disagreement.md:7](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/quantity-disagreement.md:7). The paper needs a systematic prompt-sensitivity exercise, not a single ad hoc fix.

**Minor Comments**

- The manuscript does not report the decoding/sampling settings that generate the across-run variation. That is a major omission given that repeated-run dispersion is central to the estimand.
- The center and interval objects are not fully aligned: the pooled interval comes from the mixture distribution, but the “pooled point estimate” is just the mean of run-level points [paper.qmd:100](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:100). The paper should justify that hybrid choice.
- The abstract and data section emphasize the full `3,591 / 3,630` mixed-panel run count [paper.qmd:10](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:10), [paper.qmd:124](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd:124), but the headline claims are based on the 9-quantity canonical panel. Report the effective sample size for the headline analysis just as prominently.
- `Mean |point|` in Table 1 is not economically interpretable and is never really used in the text [model-overview.md:3](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview.md:3).
- `grok-4.20` has a materially lower success rate in both the canonical and simulation tables [model-overview.md:6](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview.md:6), [model-overview-simulation.md:6](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview-simulation.md:6). The paper should discuss whether failures are random.
- The working draft still lists missing methods and benchmark sections [draft.md:259](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/draft.md:259), which matches my view that the paper is not yet publication-ready.

**Concrete Requests for Revision / Additional Analyses**

1. Reframe the estimand precisely as a prompt-and-sampling-conditioned elicited distribution, unless the authors can show robustness across prompt variants and decoding regimes.
2. Replace the global Table 1 ranking with domain-specific summaries: labor/tax, trade, production, intertemporal preference, and simulation-facing coefficients.
3. Add at least one explicit public-finance exercise that translates parameter differences into a policy-relevant object.
4. Benchmark model distributions against literature ranges or expert-elicited distributions quantity by quantity, so “economically meaningful” disagreement has an external scale.
5. Run systematic robustness checks for prompt wording, sign conventions, and interpretation-sensitive quantities, and report the exact quantity prompts in an appendix.
6. Report separation using interval overlap, pairwise exceedance probabilities, or similar measures, not just spread in pooled centers.
