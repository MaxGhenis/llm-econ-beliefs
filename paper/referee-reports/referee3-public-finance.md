# Referee Report

**Recommendation:** Major revision

This is a thoughtful and increasingly disciplined paper, but in its current form I do not think the economics is yet sharp enough for a public-finance journal. The manuscript has improved materially by redefining the estimand as a prompt-conditioned response distribution rather than a deep psychological "belief." Even so, the headline labor-tax ranking, the benchmark exercise, and the toy policy mapping still overstate how much economically interpretable structure has been recovered.

## Main concerns

1. **The paper's estimand is better framed than before, but the reported object is still a joint property of the model and the elicitation stack, not a clean economic object.** The manuscript now explicitly says it studies "prompt-conditioned elicited response distributions" rather than latent beliefs ([paper.qmd](../paper.qmd), Introduction; Design > Prompting target). That is the right move. But cross-model comparability is still blurred by the generation protocol: all models are run at `temperature = 1.0`, OpenAI uses batched draws up to `n = 8`, and providers use different structured-output mechanisms ([paper.qmd](../paper.qmd), Design > Generation protocol and exclusions). For an economics audience, that means the reported ordering is not just "what model X thinks about ETI" or "what model Y thinks about Frisch." It is what a model-plus-provider-specific-inference-stack returns under this exact protocol. The manuscript acknowledges this only partially.

2. **The headline labor-and-tax ranking is not yet a coherent public-finance summary statistic.** Table 1 averages absolute-value ranks across six quite different objects: Frisch, Marshallian, and income elasticities; the extensive-margin elasticity for single mothers; ETI; and the capital-gains realizations elasticity ([model-overview-labor-tax.md](../tables/model-overview-labor-tax.md)). Those parameters do not play symmetric roles in sufficient-statistics tax formulas, they are defined on different populations and margins, and one of them, capital-gains realizations, is not naturally part of the same labor-tax behavioral block. Using absolute values makes the aggregation less interpretable still, because it suppresses sign and object heterogeneity. I therefore do not think the manuscript has yet earned the claim that lower average elasticities on this mixed panel imply a more redistribution-permissive policy posture ([paper.qmd](../paper.qmd), Main Descriptive Results > Rankings depend on domain).

3. **The prompt-sensitivity evidence implies more identification fragility than the main text is willing to live with.** The strongest result in the paper may actually be that seemingly modest wording changes move economically important quantities a great deal. In the income-elasticity rerun, `Grok 4.1 Fast` moves from `+0.09` to `-0.097`, and several sign-fixed intervals still place nontrivial mass near or above zero ([income-signfix-delta.md](../tables/income-signfix-delta.md)). In the Armington clarification follow-up, several models shift by `0.2` to `0.57`, including `Claude Sonnet 4.6` and `Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite` ([armington-clarify-delta.md](../tables/armington-clarify-delta.md)). More generally, the canonical disagreement table shows a capital-gains realizations range from `-0.8` to `0.793`, which is not a small difference in emphasis but evidence that some objects are not yet stably identified ([quantity-disagreement.md](../tables/quantity-disagreement.md)). The paper says this is "part of the estimand," but then still leans on cross-model economic comparisons that read as more stable than the robustness tables justify.

4. **The benchmark-comparison exercise is too loose to do the reassuring work the manuscript asks of it.** Table 3 is transparent that the review intervals are hand-coded and rough ([benchmark-comparison-labor-tax.md](../tables/benchmark-comparison-labor-tax.md)). That honesty is welcome. But the inference then drawn in the text, namely that "most labor-and-tax centers lie inside rough review ranges" ([paper.qmd](../paper.qmd), Main Descriptive Results > Most labor-and-tax centers lie inside rough review ranges), is not very informative. Broad, hand-coded benchmark bands set a low bar, and the exercise compares only pooled centers to benchmark ranges rather than comparing full model uncertainty to the underlying literature uncertainty. In public-finance terms, I still do not know whether these model outputs are close to expert priors, close to review articles, or merely close to a generously drawn envelope.

5. **The ETI-to-top-rate mapping is a useful illustration, but it should not be presented as more than a classroom-style translation.** Table 4 maps each pooled ETI into `tau* = 1 / (1 + a e)` with `a = 1.5` and zero top social welfare weight ([toy-top-rate-labor-tax.md](../tables/toy-top-rate-labor-tax.md)). That is a valid toy sufficient-statistics exercise, and I appreciate the manuscript's candor in calling it stylized ([paper.qmd](../paper.qmd), Main Descriptive Results > A toy public-finance mapping). But as currently used it still overstates policy relevance. The mapping uses only ETI, ignores uncertainty around `tau*`, fixes a single Pareto parameter and welfare weight, and does not show whether the `8.1` percentage-point spread is large or small relative to within-model predictive uncertainty or ordinary empirical disagreement. As a result, the toy mapping helps intuition, but it does not yet establish that the models imply meaningfully different public-finance conclusions.

## Strengths

- **The paper is much clearer about its estimand than most work in this area.** Replacing broad language about "beliefs" with the narrower notion of prompt-conditioned response distributions is a real improvement and makes the paper more honest about what is and is not identified.
- **Separating labor-and-tax from macro-and-trade is the right direction.** This substantially improves the paper's economic coherence relative to a single pooled elasticity ranking and makes the results easier to interpret as public-finance evidence.
- **The manuscript is unusually transparent about fragility.** The income sign-fix and Armington clarification tables are exactly the kinds of robustness probes this literature needs, and the author deserves credit for foregrounding them rather than hiding them.
- **The data collection is systematic and replicable.** Eleven models, fifteen repeated runs per cell, structured outputs, failure accounting, and explicit cost reporting make this a serious descriptive dataset rather than an anecdotal prompt-comparison exercise.

## Single highest-value revision

If the author wants this to read as a public-finance paper rather than a general LLM-elicitation paper, the highest-value revision is to **rebuild the headline contribution around a narrowly defined labor-tax sufficient-statistics core and drop the average absolute-rank summary as the main result**. Concretely, I would center the paper on ETI plus a clearly justified subset of labor-supply elasticities, explain exactly how each parameter enters a common policy object, propagate the elicited uncertainty into that policy object, and demote the capital-gains and macro/trade material to secondary status. That would sharply improve estimand discipline, policy interpretation, and overall fit for a public-finance journal.
