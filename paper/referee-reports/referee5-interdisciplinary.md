Recommendation: Major revision

This is an interesting and potentially publishable paper, but in its current form it is more compelling as a well-executed project memo than as a broad economics / policy / computational social science article, because the narrative still does not cleanly separate the paper's strongest contribution from its many secondary moving parts.

Main concerns

1. The headline contribution is still too diffuse for a broad audience. The manuscript now does a better job than earlier versions of narrowing the estimand to "prompt-conditioned response distributions" rather than latent beliefs, but the paper still tries to do too many things at once: introduce a new elicitation protocol, compare 11 models, separate labor-and-tax from macro-and-trade, benchmark against rough literature ranges, map ETI into a toy top-rate formula, document prompt sensitivity, discuss tool-use robustness, and preserve simulation-facing coefficients and legacy rows for transparency. Each of these is defensible, but together they weaken the central story rather than sharpen it. For a broad journal, the paper needs one clean headline result and a simpler narrative spine. The diffusion is visible already in the abstract and opening framing in [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L9), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L17), and the later build-out of multiple result types in [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L188).

2. The paper is strongest when it emphasizes domain-specific model differences, but it still partially frames the contribution in terms of global model ordering. The revised split between labor-and-tax and macro-and-trade is a real improvement, and, in my view, it is the paper's most broadly interesting descriptive result. But the manuscript still leads with model-ranking language that invites readers to think in terms of one-dimensional "high elasticity" versus "low elasticity" models, even though the paper's own evidence shows that these orderings flip across domains. That is exactly the kind of result that could interest a broader audience, because it cautions against simplistic ideology-like readings of model behavior. The paper should lean fully into that claim and stop foregrounding any aggregate ranking language that obscures it. See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L12), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L29), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L161), [model-overview-labor-tax.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview-labor-tax.md#L1), and [model-overview-macro-trade.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/model-overview-macro-trade.md#L1).

3. The manuscript still reads as if it is written partly for people already invested in the repo and workflow, not only for journal readers. There is repeated emphasis on legacy rows, archived tool arms, appendix robustness checks, API cost, and implementation-facing distinctions that are useful for transparency but not all equally useful for persuasion. Broad-audience readers typically reward discipline over completeness. The current manuscript often chooses completeness. That is especially visible in the design and limitations sections, where the paper sounds self-aware and careful, but not yet fully editorialized into a journal article. See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L61), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L63), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L246), and [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L257).

4. The most compelling cross-cutting result may be prompt sensitivity, but it is still positioned as an important robustness check rather than as a central substantive finding. Table 5 is striking: a simple sign clarification moves `Grok 4.1 Fast` on the canonical income elasticity from `+0.09` to `-0.097`, and the text appropriately interprets this as evidence of identification fragility rather than simple model disagreement. For a broad interdisciplinary audience, this may be more arresting than many of the rank-order comparisons, because it says something concrete about how fragile apparently policy-relevant AI outputs can be. At present, the paper acknowledges this but does not yet organize itself around it. See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L216) and [income-signfix-delta.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/income-signfix-delta.md#L1).

5. The broad-audience policy relevance is plausible but still somewhat underdemonstrated. The new toy ETI-to-top-rate mapping helps, and I think including it is the right move. But outside specialists, readers will want a clearer answer to "why should I care that these models give different elasticity distributions?" The paper gestures toward research assistance, policy assistance, and synthetic expertise, but the strongest persuasive move would be to frame one or two concrete user-facing implications much earlier and more simply. Right now the policy payoff is present, but still somewhat downstream of the methods. See [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L27), [paper.qmd](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/paper.qmd#L200), and [toy-top-rate-labor-tax.md](/Users/maxghenis/llm-econ-beliefs/paper/tables/toy-top-rate-labor-tax.md#L1).

Strengths

1. The paper has a real and timely idea. Asking what distributions models return for contested economic parameters is a clear and novel contribution that sits naturally between expert elicitation, model auditing, and computational social science.

2. The manuscript is much more disciplined than many papers in this area about the estimand. The shift to "prompt-conditioned response distributions" is a substantive improvement and makes the study easier to defend.

3. The labor-and-tax versus macro-and-trade split is genuinely interesting and, in my view, the strongest broad-audience result in the paper. It offers a more nuanced and credible claim than a single global model ideology ranking.

4. The empirical execution is serious. The repeated-elicitation design, explicit pooling rule, benchmark comparison table, and prompt-sensitivity rerun make this feel like a real measurement exercise rather than a pile of anecdotes.

Single highest-value revision

Rewrite the paper around one narrower headline claim: repeated elicitation reveals domain-specific and prompt-sensitive differences in how frontier LLMs answer policy-relevant elasticity questions. Concretely, I would restructure the manuscript so that the labor-and-tax versus macro-and-trade split and the income-elasticity sign-clarification result become the core narrative, while most of the remaining workflow detail, archived tool results, and implementation-facing material move decisively into appendices. That single editorial choice would do the most to make the paper feel publishable beyond specialists.
